# CKF Methodology v1

**Framework:** Career Knowledge Framework (CKF)  
**Version:** 1.0  
**Status:** Draft - Methodology Specification

---

## Purpose

The CKF Methodology explains how career knowledge is created, refined, governed, and transformed throughout the Career Knowledge Framework.

CKF is a knowledge lifecycle rather than a document creation workflow. Its purpose is not simply to produce resumes, profiles, or career artifacts. Its purpose is to establish an authoritative, traceable representation of a person's career knowledge before that knowledge is transformed into presentation-specific outputs.

The methodology defines how experience becomes structured knowledge, how structured knowledge becomes capability patterns and insights, how those patterns are assessed, and how governed knowledge is transformed into downstream career artifacts.

---

## Methodology Scope

This methodology applies across the full CKF lifecycle:

- Capturing experience.
- Deriving capability patterns and insights.
- Assessing capability meaning, strength, transferability, and limits.
- Transforming governed knowledge into presentation artifacts.
- Receiving feedback and new information from downstream work.
- Reconciling new information into the correct upstream artifact.
- Regenerating downstream artifacts from updated authoritative knowledge.

The methodology covers how CKF should be used and maintained as a documentation-first knowledge framework. It does not define schemas, implementation code, tooling, storage formats, automation, or repository structure.

---

## Foundational Principles

CKF is governed by several foundational principles:

- A career should have an authoritative knowledge representation before it has a presentation.
- The individual is the primary authority regarding their own experience.
- Evidence strengthens transparency and confidence but does not create experience authority.
- Upstream artifacts control downstream artifacts.
- Downstream artifacts are derivative and must not override upstream authority.
- Traceability should be preserved across the lifecycle.
- Unsupported claims should be reconciled upstream, qualified, or omitted.
- Career knowledge improves iteratively through revision, reconciliation, and regeneration.

The core authority flow is:

```text
MEL -> CPIL -> CCA -> RGF
```

The authority distinctions are:

- MEL = experience authority.
- CPIL = capability pattern and insight authority.
- CCA = interpretation and assessment authority.
- RGF = presentation transformation authority.

---

## Knowledge Lifecycle

The CKF knowledge lifecycle begins with the individual and continues through governed artifacts, downstream presentation, feedback, and upstream reconciliation.

```text
Individual
      |
      v
Experience
      |
      v
MEL
      |
      v
CPIL
      |
      v
CCA
      |
      v
RGF
      |
      v
Career Artifact
      |
      v
Feedback / New Information
      |
      v
Reconcile Upstream
      |
      +-----------------> regenerate downstream
```

This lifecycle is iterative rather than strictly linear. Downstream work often reveals missing experience, unclear capability patterns, unsupported interpretations, or presentation constraints. Those discoveries should not be patched into the downstream artifact as new authority. They should be reconciled into the appropriate upstream artifact and then used to regenerate downstream materials.

The result is continuous refinement of authoritative career knowledge rather than continual editing of disconnected resumes.

---

## Framework Workflow

The CKF workflow moves through four governed stages:

1. Experience Capture in the Master Experience Ledger (MEL).
2. Capability Pattern & Insight Derivation in the Capability Pattern & Insight Ledger (CPIL).
3. Capability Assessment in the Career Capability Assessment (CCA).
4. Presentation Transformation in the Resume Generation Framework (RGF).

Each stage consumes upstream information and produces governed knowledge for downstream use.

Each stage must preserve its authority boundary. The MEL records experience. The CPIL derives capability patterns and insights. The CCA interprets and assesses those patterns and insights. The RGF transforms upstream-authorized knowledge into presentation-specific artifacts.

---

## Stage 1 — Experience Capture (MEL)

Experience capture records what the individual has done, where it occurred, when it occurred, and what context gives that experience meaning.

The MEL should capture experience such as:

- Professional employment.
- Self-employment.
- Independent consulting.
- Volunteer work.
- Education and structured learning.
- Professional development.
- Apprenticeships.
- Independent research.
- Open-source contributions.
- Personal projects.
- Publications.
- Creative work.
- Other experience that contributes to professional capability.

The MEL should preserve relevant context, dates, responsibilities, activities, outcomes, roles, organizations, and supporting evidence where available.

The MEL is not an assessment artifact and not a resume. It should record experience without converting it into unsupported capability claims, career conclusions, or presentation language.

New experience discovered downstream returns to the MEL before it is used authoritatively.

---

## Stage 2 — Capability Pattern & Insight Derivation (CPIL)

Capability Pattern & Insight Derivation translates MEL experience records into structured capability patterns and insights.

The CPIL identifies what capabilities are reasonably supported by recorded experience. It may derive capability patterns from responsibilities, activities, outcomes, tools, methods, domains, repeated behaviors, and reasonable implications of MEL entries.

CPIL derivation should be:

- Traceable to MEL records.
- Conservative in scope.
- Clear about context and limits.
- Distinct from assessment and presentation.
- Reusable by downstream artifacts.

The CPIL should not invent capabilities, inflate capability scope, or treat aspirations as demonstrated capability.

New capability patterns discovered downstream return to the CPIL before they are used authoritatively. If a new capability depends on experience not yet represented in the MEL, that experience must first be reconciled into the MEL.

---

## Stage 3 — Capability Assessment (CCA)

Capability assessment interprets what CPIL capability patterns and insights indicate about professional strength, depth, breadth, transferability, specialization, progression, relevance, confidence, and limits.

The CCA may assess:

- Capability strength.
- Capability maturity.
- Depth and breadth.
- Transferability across roles or domains.
- Recency and continuity.
- Career or role alignment.
- Specialization or generalization.
- Evidence quality and confidence.
- Gaps, constraints, and uncertainty.

The CCA must remain grounded in CPIL records and relevant MEL context. It may interpret and assess, but it must not create new experience authority or new capability pattern authority.

New interpretations discovered during presentation or downstream artifact work return to the CCA before they are treated as authoritative. If a new interpretation depends on a new capability pattern, that capability must first be reconciled into the CPIL.

---

## Stage 4 — Presentation Transformation (RGF)

Presentation transformation adapts governed upstream knowledge into audience-specific career artifacts.

The RGF may adapt:

- Emphasis.
- Organization.
- Wording.
- Formatting.
- Density.
- Sequencing.
- Tone.
- Artifact-specific structure.

The RGF may use MEL, CPIL, CCA, and presentation-specific context. It may tailor how authorized knowledge is presented for a target opportunity, role, audience, or format.

The RGF must not invent experience, capabilities, assessments, achievements, credentials, outcomes, or metrics. It must not convert presentation needs into source authority.

Presentation changes belong in the RGF or the downstream presentation artifact. RGF outputs and downstream artifacts never become authoritative records.

---

## Knowledge Refinement Loop

CKF improves through an iterative refinement loop.

Downstream work may reveal:

- Missing experience.
- Incomplete experience context.
- Unclear capability patterns.
- Unsupported capability claims.
- New interpretations.
- Weak confidence or unclear limits.
- Presentation constraints.
- Audience-specific terminology needs.

The methodology requires routing each discovery to the artifact that has authority over it:

- New experience returns to the MEL.
- New capability patterns return to the CPIL.
- New interpretations return to the CCA.
- Presentation changes remain in the RGF.

After upstream reconciliation, downstream artifacts should be regenerated or revised from the updated authoritative knowledge.

---

## Reconciliation Process

Reconciliation is the process of moving new, corrected, ambiguous, or unsupported information to the artifact that has authority over it.

When new information appears, identify its type:

- Experience information belongs in the MEL.
- Capability pattern or insight information belongs in the CPIL.
- Interpretation or assessment information belongs in the CCA.
- Presentation information belongs in the RGF or downstream artifact.

Reconciliation should preserve traceability and avoid silent downstream authority drift. A resume bullet, profile sentence, interview answer, or career summary should not become the authoritative source for new experience, capability, or interpretation.

If the information cannot be reconciled because it lacks support, it should be omitted, qualified, or flagged for clarification rather than treated as authoritative.

---

## Traceability Throughout the Lifecycle

Traceability connects each downstream claim to its upstream basis.

Experience claims should trace to MEL records.

Capability patterns and insights should trace to MEL records through CPIL entries.

Interpretations and assessments should trace to CPIL records and relevant MEL context.

Presentation claims should trace to MEL, CPIL, and CCA support as appropriate.

Traceability does not require every presentation artifact to expose every source reference to its audience. It does require that substantive claims remain explainable and recoverable within the CKF lifecycle.

---

## Governance Throughout the Lifecycle

Governance applies at every stage of the methodology.

The MEL governs experience. The CPIL governs capability patterns and insights. The CCA governs interpretation and assessment. The RGF governs presentation transformation.

Upstream artifacts control downstream artifacts. Downstream artifacts may consume, transform, interpret, or present upstream material only within their authority boundary.

Governance prevents unsupported claims from entering the lifecycle, prevents downstream artifacts from overriding upstream authority, and keeps career knowledge reusable across multiple presentation contexts.

---

## Revision Methodology

Revisions should occur where authority resides.

Experience revisions should be made in the MEL.

Capability pattern and insight revisions should be made in the CPIL.

Interpretation and assessment revisions should be made in the CCA.

Presentation revisions should be made in the RGF or downstream presentation artifact.

When an upstream artifact changes, downstream artifacts should be reviewed for impact. A revised MEL entry may require CPIL updates. A revised CPIL pattern may require CCA reassessment. A revised CCA assessment may require RGF presentation changes.

Revision should improve the authoritative knowledge base, not merely patch a downstream artifact.

---

## Working with Evidence

Evidence supports the CKF lifecycle by improving transparency, specificity, and confidence.

Evidence may include employment records, certificates, licenses, publications, repositories, work samples, portfolios, presentations, images, videos, documentation, or other supporting materials.

Evidence strengthens records but does not create experience authority. The individual remains the primary authority regarding their own experience, and the MEL remains the governed artifact for representing that experience.

When evidence reveals new experience, corrections, or additional context, that information should be reconciled into the MEL before it is used authoritatively downstream.

Evidence may influence confidence and limits in CPIL and CCA, and it may affect how RGF presents a claim. It must not be used to bypass upstream authority.

---

## Confidence and Uncertainty

CKF should preserve confidence, limits, and uncertainty where they materially affect knowledge or presentation.

Confidence may reflect:

- Specificity of records.
- Recency and duration.
- Repetition across experiences.
- Directness of support.
- Quality and relevance of evidence.
- Clarity of outcomes.
- Degree of inference required.

Uncertainty should be preserved when support is incomplete, ambiguous, indirect, narrow, outdated, or context-specific.

The methodology should not resolve uncertainty through invention. If a claim cannot be supported, it should be reconciled upstream, qualified, or omitted.

---

## Downstream Artifact Generation

Downstream artifact generation uses governed upstream knowledge to produce practical career materials.

Downstream artifacts may include:

- Resumes.
- Professional profiles.
- LinkedIn content.
- Cover letter source material.
- Interview preparation materials.
- Career summaries.
- Professional biographies.
- Portfolios.
- Job application materials.
- Future career artifacts.

Downstream artifacts are generated from authoritative knowledge; they do not become authoritative knowledge.

When downstream artifact generation reveals missing or improved knowledge, the methodology routes that information upstream for reconciliation before regeneration.

---

## Future Framework Evolution

CKF may evolve to include additional artifact types, methods, evidence sources, presentation channels, or analysis layers.

Future evolution should preserve the established authority model unless governance explicitly defines a new bounded authority role.

New framework components should identify:

- What authority they consume.
- What authority, if any, they hold.
- What artifacts they depend on.
- What artifacts depend on them.
- How traceability is preserved.
- How unsupported claims are handled.
- How revisions are reconciled.

Future expansion should strengthen the lifecycle rather than collapse experience, capability, assessment, and presentation into a single artifact.

---

## Summary

The CKF Methodology describes a career knowledge lifecycle.

The lifecycle begins with the individual, records experience in the MEL, derives capability patterns and insights in the CPIL, assesses those patterns in the CCA, transforms governed knowledge through the RGF, and produces downstream career artifacts.

CKF is iterative rather than linear. New experience returns to the MEL. New capability patterns return to the CPIL. New interpretations return to the CCA. Presentation changes belong in the RGF.

Downstream artifacts never become authoritative. They are generated from governed career knowledge and should be regenerated when upstream knowledge changes.

The methodology emphasizes continuous refinement of authoritative knowledge rather than continual editing of resumes.
